1. Statement of Purpose

My interest lies right at the intersection of mathematics, programming, and geography. My current academic journey fortunately fulfills all three, which has been the fundamental drive behind my ideas and my urge to advance my research. I would like to pursue a PhD to contribute to atmospheric science through machine learning alongside established researchers.

I was first introduced to machine learning in 2019 when I took a class taught by my academic adviser, Dr. Peter Sadowski. As someone who enjoys programming and mathematics, I instantly knew machine learning was the path I had been looking for. Since then, Dr. Sadowski has given me multiple opportunities to work on machine learning projects, most of them related to atmospheric science. In 2022, I used neural networks to downscale coarse atmospheric variables to monthly rainfall at individual weather stations, for which I defended my thesis. I also used state-of-the-art machine learning methods to predict high-resolution cloud cover maps from global atmospheric models. In 2023, I worked as a contractor to predict the radiation budget using data obtained from real-time satellites. These works partially led to multiple funding awards through my PI, totaling over a million dollars.

Since 2024, I have worked on an innovative statistical downscaling method that enables gridded rainfall maps using a machine learning model trained only on off-grid, sparse weather stations. I am also currently applying a flow model to downscale weather predictions from 30 km to 1 km. Another ongoing research project aims to enable kernel methods to incorporate complex topography for observational interpolation. All of these works have extended beyond the Computer Science department to close collaborations with the Water Resources Research Center and the Atmospheric Science Department.

Aside from these research activities, I believe the past years have prepared me well for the commitment required in PhD research. I have had the privilege of taking classes from other departments to deepen my knowledge beyond Computer Science, such as Real Analysis (MATH), Random Processes (EE), and various atmospheric science courses (ATMO), all of which have strengthened my foundation as a researcher at the intersection of machine learning and atmospheric science.

Hawai‘i is a perfect place for conducting atmospheric research because of the unique challenges it presents and the cultural and environmental significance of its results to the islands. The complex topography complicates physical processes involving precipitation, temperature, wind, and many other atmospheric phenomena, making it difficult to accurately capture and model these dynamics. The remote and isolated environment poses challenges in both resource management—such as groundwater, solar energy, food, and forestry—and ecosystem vulnerability, showing the importance of advancing research that helps address these issues. Ultimately, studying these challenges in Hawai‘i offers not only scientific insight but also a direct opportunity to contribute to the resilience and sustainability of the islands.

I am eager to continue this journey through doctoral study and to contribute to advancing data-driven approaches in atmospheric research.

3. Evidence of Scholarly Ability

M.S. Plan A Thesis

  • Machine Learning Based Statistical Downscaling for Rainfall on Hawaiian Islands. 2022.

    Manuscript available through [ScholarSpace]

    I investigated the applicability of Machine Learning methods (Neural Networks, XGBoost) in statistically downscaling coarse atmospheric variables to monthly rainfall observations at sparse weather stations in Hawaiian islands. I tested the methods against Linear Regression baseline, as well as conducting ablation study and hyperparameter optimization. This work has become the foundation for the subsequent work (Statistical Downscaling from Sparse Observations with Deep Learning for Monthly Rainfall Projections in Hawai`i, as listed below.)

Technical Report

  • Yusuke Hatanaka, Yannik Glaser, Geoff Galgon, Giuseppe Torri, Peter Sadowski. Diffusion Models for High-resolution Solar Forecasts. 2023.

    First author. Cited by 27 as of 2025. Manuscript available at [arXiv]

    I implemented the cascated diffusion model to downscale coarse atmospheric variables down to sub-km resolution over the island of Oahu. I also automated the pipeline to download and preprocess data from GOES (Geostationary Operational Environmental Satellites). This work is publicly recognized as one of the first researches to apply diffusion models in weather forecasting settings (link to github repo, Awesome-Diffusion-Models-for-Weather-Forecasting)

  • Yusuke Hatanaka, Amila Indika, Thomas Giambelluca, Peter Sadowski . Statistical Downscaling from Sparse Observations with Deep Learning for Mean Monthly Rainfall Projections in Hawai`i. 2025.

    • First author. Submitted to Artificial Intelligence for the Earth Systems in 2024. Currently under revision.

    I designed a new approach to downscaling coarse atmospheric variables to sub-km grids using Neural Networks. Building on the previous work of mine (Machine Learning Based Statitical Downscaling for Rainfall on Hawaiian Islands), I implemented and tested an innovative neural network architecture that allows to learn from sparse observations and make monthly rainfall predictions at any sub-km gridpoints.
